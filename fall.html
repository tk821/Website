1516 – 1517 CE  ·  The End

Two Battles,
Two Centuries Erased

In less than a year, Sultan Selim I's Ottoman army dismantled a sultanate that had repulsed the Mongols, expelled the Crusaders, and stood for two and a half centuries.

Prelude to Conflict

Selim I and the Ottoman Ambition

By 1516, Sultan Selim I — known as Yavuz, "the Grim" — had already consolidated Ottoman power to a degree not seen since Mehmed the Conqueror. He had neutralized the Safavid Persians at the Battle of Chaldiran (1514), again using superior artillery to devastating effect. Now he turned south.

The pretext for conflict was the Mamluk Sultan Qansuh al-Ghawri's diplomatic overtures toward the Safavids — a power the Ottomans regarded as an existential enemy. Whether or not the accusations of Mamluk treachery were accurate, Selim needed little justification for a war of conquest he had almost certainly already decided to fight.

The contrast between the two armies in 1516 was a near-perfect illustration of everything the preceding century had made possible. The Ottoman forces included the feared Janissary infantry with their matchlock muskets, and an artillery train with hundreds of field cannon. The Mamluk army was still organized around heavy cavalry — magnificent horsemen, but armed with bows and lances against firearms.

August 24, 1516

The Battle of Marj Dabiq

The two armies met on the plain of Marj Dabiq, north of Aleppo in Syria. Sultan Qansuh al-Ghawri, at nearly 80 years old and reportedly in failing health, commanded the Mamluk forces personally — a measure of both his courage and the desperation of his situation.

The Ottoman guns spoke first. And last. The Mamluk cavalry charged into a wall of fire they had no answer for.

— Summary of the Battle of Marj Dabiq, August 1516

The battle was decided with brutal efficiency. Ottoman artillery and musket fire tore through the Mamluk cavalry charges. The Mamluk right wing, commanded by the governor of Aleppo Khair Bey, reportedly defected to the Ottomans at a critical moment. The left wing collapsed. Sultan Qansuh al-Ghawri suffered a fatal stroke on the battlefield. The entire Syrian province fell to Selim within days.

January 22, 1517

The Battle of Ridaniyya — The Final Stand

A new sultan, Tuman bay II, rallied what remained of the Mamluk military for a final defense of Egypt. He demonstrated genuine tactical ingenuity, positioning Mamluk cannon in prepared earthworks outside Cairo and training his troops in their use. It was the most serious Mamluk attempt at gunpowder warfare — but it came a generation too late.

What Tuman Bay Did Right

He positioned artillery to channel the Ottoman advance. He chose defensible terrain. He attempted to use the Cairo street-network for guerrilla defense after the field battle. He fought with remarkable personal bravery throughout.

Why It Was Not Enough

The Ottoman artillery outranged and outmassed his guns. His cannon crews lacked the training and doctrine of the Ottoman artillerists. When Khair Bey — the same defector from Marj Dabiq — guided Ottoman forces around the Mamluk flank, the defense collapsed.

Cairo fell on January 22, 1517. Tuman bay II fled, continued guerrilla resistance for weeks, and was ultimately captured and hanged at the Bab Zuwayla gate of Cairo — one of the great Mamluk-era monuments — on April 13, 1517. With his death, the Mamluk Sultanate formally ceased to exist.

Historical Assessment

What the Fall Teaches Us

The collapse of the Mamluk Sultanate was not inevitable, but it was not accidental either. It was the product of identifiable choices — and, crucially, of choices not made.

Three Interlocking Causes of Decline

1. Military Cultural Conservatism

The Mamluk warrior class identified so deeply with cavalry-based warfare that the adoption of firearms threatened their social identity and political power. The state's military culture could not be reformed without dismantling the class that ran the state.

2. Political Instability

With an average sultanate of less than four years during the Burji period, no ruler could accumulate the political capital and time horizon necessary for generational reform. The result was a state perpetually in crisis management.

3. Economic Disruption

The Portuguese circumnavigation of Africa severed the Mamluks' greatest strategic asset — their control of the Eurasian trade routes. As revenues fell, so did the capacity for military investment that might have extended the Sultanate's life.

The Mamluks were not defeated because they were weak. They were defeated because their greatest strengths belonged to a world that had ceased to exist.

Legacy

What the Mamluks Left Behind

The end of the Mamluk Sultanate did not mean the end of Mamluk culture or even Mamluk power entirely. Under the Ottomans, a class of Mamluk beys continued to govern Egypt, maintaining considerable local authority until Napoleon's invasion in 1798.

Their architectural legacy is extraordinary: hundreds of mosques, madrasas, khanqahs, and mausoleums throughout Cairo, Jerusalem, and Damascus represent some of the finest Islamic architecture ever built. Cairo's historic district, centered on the street of al-Muizz, is a living testament to Mamluk patronage.

As a historical case study, the Mamluk decline offers lessons that transcend its era: about the dangers of confusing present strength with permanent advantage, the political difficulty of reform in entrenched hierarchies, and the vulnerability that comes from economic dependence on a single strategic asset.

They repelled the Mongols. They ended the Crusades. And they fell to a force they saw coming and could not bring themselves to become.
